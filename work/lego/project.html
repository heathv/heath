Problem

Scientists at the Max Planck University in Leipzig, Germany have been studying the behavior of chimpanzees in West Africa through the use of motion-activated video cameras. When activated, whether by chimps, warthogs, or nothing at all, these cameras automatically record 3 minutes of video. This has resulted in over 7,000 hours of video so far. In order for the four-person science team to analyze the footage, each would have to view over 1,750 hours of video.

Solution

Our team at the Zooniverse worked with the Mac Planck scientists to create a data reduction process that crowd-sources the task. The result is a simple web interface that allows anyone to sort through the data, identify what activated the camera, and what it's behaviors are.

This process involves breaking each 3 minute video into twelve, 15-second clips. These shorter clips are easier for users to process in large numbers, and allow for a wider array of subject matter to be analyzed in a short period. Each clip is viewed five times, and if the majority agree on what's in the image it is pulled from the set and retired. The goal for the project to give the science team a fully-analyzed dataset that tells them what, if anything, is in each video, and what it's doing. This saves them months of work in the end!
